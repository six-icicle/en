01
Current — color-mix bg, black 35%
color-mix(in oklch, var(--bg), black 35%)
en
— canvas —
Heavy. Bar reads ~35% darker than canvas, sinking below the floor while tiles sit above. Inverts the lifted-bg recipe the rest of the app follows. The "too dark" instinct is right.
02
Lifted — chrome floats above canvas
color-mix(in oklch, var(--bg), white 6%)
en
— canvas —
Mirrors the lifted-bg theme recipe. Chrome sits 6% lighter than canvas, the same direction tiles move. Reads as "the bar is part of the lifted layer." Soft separation, no weight. Strong default candidate.
03
Tile-flush — chrome IS a tile
var(--tile)
en
— canvas —
Reuses the existing tile token. The bar reads like a wide tile pinned at the top. Quiet, but loses the "this is chrome" cue — feels almost too unified.
04
Frosted glass — same recipe as the buttons
linear-gradient(white 5% → 2%) over var(--bg) + blur(20px)
en
— canvas —
Bar uses the same gradient as the buttons themselves. Side-effect: buttons disappear into the bar — they share a substrate. Either visually elegant ("one frosted slab") or too unified (no chrome/control distinction). Better when there's content scrolling underneath.
05
Subtle gradient — atmospheric depth
linear-gradient(var(--bg) → var(--bg)+8% black)
en
— canvas —
Starts at canvas, ends 8% darker. Reads as "lit from above" without committing to a hard color shift. Brand-coherent (you already use 180° gradients in the buttons). Compromise candidate.
06
Hairline only — canvas-flush, no fill
background: transparent; border-bottom: 1px var(--line-2)
en
— canvas —
Maximum quiet. Bar dissolves into the canvas; only the buttons + hairline mark its territory. Tea-ceremony minimalism in its purest form. Risk: when textures get heavy (sakura rain, scanlines) the bar can feel like "where do I click?"
07
Foot-bar twin — top + bottom symmetry
linear-gradient(var(--bg) → var(--tile-2))
en
— canvas —
Mirror of the foot bar's recipe — your explicit ask. Top and bottom bookend the canvas with the same gradient, except the foot reads bg→tile-2 (lighter at bottom) and this reads the same direction (canvas dark above, tile-2 toward the seam). Visually frames the workspace. Risk: gradient direction is the same as foot, so they don't oppose; they both feel like they're "leading into" the canvas. Could flip the navbar gradient (tile-2 → bg) for a true mirror.

recommendation — 13 variants

Top three for an A/B/C eyeball:

  • 02 Lifted — strict obedience to the lifted-bg design law. One token, retints across 8 themes, smallest possible change.
  • 09 Engawa beam [frontend-design] — same color as canvas + drop shadow + top highlight. The bar is held above the workspace by light, not tone. Most architecturally interesting.
  • 10 Sumi ink wash [impeccable] — gradient from bg+ink-4 22% to bg. Ink fading on washi. The most poetic option, still strictly on-strategy.

Honorable mentions:

  • 08 Tea-stain [frontend-design] — accent infused into chrome. Most theme-expressive; if you want themes to feel more different from each other, this is the lever.
  • 13 Embossed channel [ui-ux-pro-max] — soft neumorphism, recessed groove. Adds depth without color shift; closest to "premium hardware" feel.
  • 11 Folded-paper seam [impeccable] — double-line border, zero color shift. Pure architecture. Quieter cousin of 06.

Probably skip:

  • 01 Current — the reason we're doing this exercise.
  • 04 Frosted — buttons share the bar's substrate, dissolve into it.
  • 06 Hairline — too quiet under heavy textures.
  • 07 Foot twin — top + bottom symmetry doubles chrome weight; foot is a footnote, not a beam.
  • 12 Bento beam — tile-2 reads like a hover state stuck on; brand-loud.

If you want one decisive call: 10 Sumi ink wash is the most refined of the 13 and obeys both the lifted-bg recipe (top edge sits up from canvas via the ink-4 wash, not down via black-mix) and the chroma-restraint rule. 09 Engawa is the runner-up if you'd rather the bar read as architectural rather than tonal. 02 Lifted remains the smallest-diff, lowest-risk option.
